Eclipse Overview

The total lunar eclipse of September 7, 2025, is one of the year's most significant astronomical events. The Moon will pass deep into Earth's umbral shadow, creating an intensely red "Blood Moon" during the 82-minute totality phase. Occurring near perigee, the Moon will appear larger than average, enhancing the visual spectacle.

The Science of a Blood Moon

During a total lunar eclipse, Earth's atmosphere filters sunlight passing through it. It scatters blue light but allows red light to pass through and be refracted, or bent, toward the Moon. This effect, called Rayleigh scattering, is the same phenomenon that creates colorful sunrises and sunsets. The exact color of the eclipsed Moon—from bright orange to deep blood-red—depends on global atmospheric conditions like cloud cover and dust.

Global Impact & Photography

Population Impact

An estimated 7.03 billion people (88% of the world's population) will be able to see at least a part of this eclipse, making it one of the most widely observable astronomical events in recent history.

Observation Tips

Unlike a solar eclipse, a lunar eclipse is perfectly safe to view without special eye protection. For the best experience, find a location away from city lights. Binoculars or a small telescope will reveal stunning surface details on the reddened Moon.
